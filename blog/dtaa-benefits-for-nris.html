Understanding DTAA Benefits for NRIs

If you live abroad and earn income in India — or vice versa — you could end up paying tax on the same income in two countries. That is where the Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA) saves you significant money.

What Is DTAA?

DTAA is a tax treaty signed between India and 90+ countries. It ensures that income earned in one country is not fully taxed again in the other. India has active DTAAs with the UK, USA, Canada, Australia, UAE, Singapore, and many more. You can view the full list of India's tax treaties on the Income Tax India official portal.

Key Benefits of DTAA for NRIs

  • Reduced TDS rates on dividends, interest, and royalties
  • Exemption from double taxation on foreign income
  • Tax credit for taxes already paid abroad
  • Clarity on which country has the right to tax specific income
  • Protection from aggressive tax demands on foreign income — especially if you received a Section 148 reassessment notice
  • Reduced capital gains tax on Indian property sales under applicable treaty articles

Country-Specific DTAA Guides

Each treaty has different rates and rules. If you are based in the UK, see our dedicated India-UK DTAA guide for British NRIs. For a full strategy on structuring income across borders, read our complete cross-border tax planning guide.

How to Claim DTAA Benefits?

To claim DTAA relief, you must submit a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the country where you are a tax resident, along with Form 10F filed on the income tax portal. Your CA must correctly mention DTAA relief in your ITR to avoid erroneous demands. See also our guide on how residency status affects your tax liability — your status determines which DTAA articles apply to you.

Common Mistakes NRIs Make

  • Not submitting TRC to Indian banks, leading to higher TDS — this can be partially recovered via Section 197 lower deduction certificates
  • Claiming wrong DTAA article for income type
  • Missing Form 10F filing, invalidating the DTAA claim
  • Filing under wrong residency status, losing DTAA benefit
